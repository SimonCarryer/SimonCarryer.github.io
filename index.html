"Adventure in New Zealand" is a fascinating window into early New Zealand history, and especially the founding of Wellington. But! The author is far from an unbiased source.

"Adventure in New Zealand" was written for a few different reasons (which we'll discover as we go along), but chief among them was to promote the goals of the New Zealand Company and the Wakefield family back in Britain. That motive, along with the author's personal biases make this an extremely slanted view of events. Throughout the text I'm going to interject (in sections like this) to give different perspectives and modern interpretations of what the author is describing.

To me, this tension between the narrator's self-aggrandisement and self-justification, and the perspective of modern historical knowledge makes this an even more engaging text. I hope you'll agree.
